Need for Non-Conventional Energy Sources

  1. Environmental Concerns: Traditional energy sources like coal, oil, and natural gas contribute significantly to air and water pollution, as well as greenhouse gas emissions. Non-conventional energy sources offer cleaner alternatives, helping to mitigate climate change and reduce environmental degradation.
  1. Energy Security: Dependence on imported fossil fuels exposes countries to geopolitical risks and price fluctuations in the global market. Investing in non-conventional energy sources enhances energy security by diversifying the energy mix and reducing reliance on finite resources concentrated in specific regions.
  1. Economic Benefits: Renewable energy sources offer opportunities for economic growth and job creation, particularly in manufacturing, installation, and maintenance of renewable energy infrastructure. Investing in non-conventional energy can stimulate local economies and reduce energy import bills over the long term.
  1. Resource Conservation: Non-conventional energy sources, such as solar, wind, and hydroelectric power, harness naturally replenishing resources like sunlight, wind, and water. Unlike fossil fuels, these resources are abundant and widely distributed, reducing pressure on finite resources and promoting sustainable development.
  1. Energy Access: In many regions, particularly rural and remote areas, access to reliable electricity remains limited. Non-conventional energy sources, especially decentralized systems like solar microgrids, offer cost-effective solutions to expand energy access and improve quality of life for underserved populations.
  1. Technological Advancements: Rapid advancements in renewable energy technologies, coupled with declining costs, make non-conventional energy sources increasingly competitive with traditional fossil fuels. Continued innovation and investment in research and development further drive down costs and improve efficiency, making renewable energy an attractive option for energy generation.

Energy Alternatives

There are several energy alternatives to traditional fossil fuels, which can be broadly categorized into renewable and non-renewable sources. Here are some key energy alternatives:

1. Renewable Energy Sources

  1. Solar Energy: Harnessing sunlight through photovoltaic panels or concentrating solar power systems to generate electricity or heat water.
  1. Wind Energy: Utilizing wind turbines to convert wind energy into electricity.
  1. Hydroelectric Power: Generating electricity by harnessing the energy of flowing water through dams or run-of-river installations.
  1. Biomass Energy: Using organic materials such as wood, crop residues, or waste to produce heat, electricity, or biofuels.
  1. Geothermal Energy: Tapping into heat from the Earth's crust to generate electricity or provide heating and cooling.

2. Nuclear Energy

Utilizing nuclear reactions to generate heat, which is then used to produce steam to drive turbines for electricity generation.

Nuclear energy is considered low-carbon but raises concerns regarding safety, waste management, and proliferation.

3. Hydrogen Energy

Producing hydrogen from water electrolysis, natural gas reforming, or biomass gasification, which can be used as a clean fuel for transportation, heating, and industrial processes.

4. Tidal and Wave Energy

Capturing energy from ocean tides and waves to generate electricity.

Tidal barrages, tidal turbines, and wave energy converters are some technologies used in this field.

5. Biofuels

Liquid fuels derived from biomass, such as ethanol from sugarcane, corn, or cellulosic materials, and biodiesel from vegetable oils or animal fats.

Biofuels can be used as alternatives to gasoline and diesel in transportation.

6. Fuel Cells

Electrochemical devices that convert chemical energy directly into electricity, with hydrogen being a common fuel source.

Fuel cells have applications in transportation, stationary power generation, and portable electronics.

7. Energy Storage

Advancements in battery technologies, including lithium-ion batteries, flow batteries, and solid-state batteries, enable efficient storage of electricity from renewable sources for later use, enhancing grid stability and enabling greater integration of renewables.

Solar Radiation and Measurement

Extra-Terrestrial Radiation and its Spectral Distribution

Everything in nature emits electromagnetic energy, and solar radiation is energy emitted by the sun. The energy of extraterrestrial solar radiation is distributed over a wide continuous spectrum ranging from ultraviolet to infrared rays.

The solar spectrum can be divided in to different regions based on wavelength:

  1. Ultraviolet (UV) Radiation: This includes wavelengths shorter than visible light, typically ranging from about 10 nanometers to 400 nanometers. UV radiation is further divided into UV-A (400-315 nm), UV-B (315-280 nm), and UV-C (280-100 nm), with UV-C mostly absorbed by the Earth's atmosphere.
  1. Visible Light: This is the portion of the spectrum that is visible to the human eye and ranges from approximately 400 nanometers (violet) to 700 nanometers (red).
  1. Infrared (IR) Radiation: Beyond the visible spectrum, IR radiation ranges from about 700 nanometers to 1 millimeter. It's further divided into near-infrared (NIR), mid-infrared (MIR), and far-infrared (FIR) regions.

Pyrometer

  • A pyrometer, also known as an infrared thermometer, radiation thermometer, or non-contact thermometer, is used to detect the surface temperature of an object. This detection depends on the radiation (infrared or visible) emitted from the object.
  • Pyrometers act as photodetectors because they have the property of absorbing energy and measuring the intensity of electromagnetic waves at any wavelength.
  • These devices are used to measure high temperatures in furnaces and can do so very accurately, precisely, visually, and quickly.
  • Pyrometers are available in different spectral ranges (since metals require short-wave ranges and non-metals require long-wave ranges).

Shading Ring Pyrheliometer

  • A shading ring pyrheliometer is an instrument used to measure direct solar radiation. It specifically measures the amount of solar radiation that reaches the Earth's surface without being scattered or diffused by the atmosphere.
  • The instrument uses a shading ring to block diffuse solar radiation, allowing it to accurately measure direct sunlight.

Construction and Principle of Working

  • A shading ring pyrheliometer is designed to measure direct solar radiation by capturing sunlight in a controlled and precise manner.
  • The main body of the pyrheliometer consists of a tube that is blackened inside to absorb as much solar radiation as possible, minimizing internal reflection and ensuring that all incoming solar radiation is absorbed.
  • The tube is constructed with a specific acceptance angle, typically around 5Â°, allowing only direct solar radiation from the sun to enter while minimizing the amount of diffuse radiation that could affect the readings.
  • An alignment indicator helps ensure that the pyrheliometer is properly aligned with the sun, as precise alignment is crucial for accurately measuring direct solar radiation.
  • Inside the tube, a black absorber plate captures the incoming solar radiation, converting it into heat. The black surface of the absorber plate is highly effective at absorbing all wavelengths of light, maximizing the conversion of solar radiation into thermal energy.
  • Thermocouples attached to the absorber plate measure the temperature increase caused by the absorbed solar radiation, converting this thermal energy into an electrical signal that can be quantified.
  • Insulation around the tube and sensitive components prevents external temperature changes from affecting the accuracy of the measurements, maintaining a stable environment for the thermocouples and the absorber plate.
  • The pyrheliometer is mounted on a two-axis tracking mechanism that follows the sun's path across the sky throughout the day, ensuring continuous alignment with the sun for consistent and accurate measurements.
  • As the direct solar radiation enters the tube, it strikes the black absorber plate, causing a temperature increase that is measured by the thermocouples.
  • The electrical signal generated by the thermocouples is processed to calculate solar irradiance, with a temperature compensation mechanism adjusting the readings to account for any ambient temperature variations.
  • This setup allows for continuous monitoring and recording of direct solar radiation throughout the day as the sun moves, providing accurate and reliable data on solar energy.

Sunshine Recorder

A sunshine recorder, also known as a Campbell-Stokes recorder, is a simple yet effective device used to record the duration of sunshine at a specific location.

Construction and Principle of Working

The Campbell-Stokes Sunshine Recorder primarily consists of a glass sphere and a recorder:

  • Glass Sphere: This solid, clear glass sphere focuses the sunâ€™s rays onto a specific point. The sphere acts as a lens, concentrating sunlight onto a calibrated card or strip placed in the recorder.
  • Recorder: The recorder is typically an adjustable metal housing that holds the recording card. The card is made of heat-sensitive paper that gets scorched or burnt by the concentrated sunlight. As the sun moves across the sky, the focused light burns a trace along the card.

Working Mechanism

  • The recorder operates by focusing sunlight through the glass sphere onto the recording card.
  • When the sun is shining, the concentrated rays are strong enough to burn a mark on the card. The length of the burn trace on the card represents the duration of sunshine for that day.
  • The card is marked with hourly intervals, allowing observers to determine not only the total amount of sunshine but also the periods during which the sun was shining.
  • The device must be aligned correctly for different latitudes and times of the year, ensuring accurate measurements. By changing the position of the recording card in the housing, the recorder can adapt to seasonal changes in the sun's path.

Actinometer

  • An actinometer is a device used to measure the intensity of electromagnetic radiation, particularly in the visible and ultraviolet regions of the spectrum. It is often used to measure solar radiation.
  • The term "actinometer" is derived from "actino," meaning light, and "meter," meaning to measure.
  • There are various types of actinometers, but they generally work based on the principle of photochemical reactions. In a basic actinometer, a photosensitive substance undergoes a chemical reaction when exposed to light, and the extent of this reaction is proportional to the intensity of the light. This reaction can be measured quantitatively to determine the intensity of the radiation.
  • Actinometers are used in atmospheric science, climatology, and photometry to measure solar radiation, study the Earth's energy budget, and monitor changes in solar irradiance over time.

Bolometer

  • A bolometer is a device used to measure the power of incident electromagnetic radiation, typically in the infrared or microwave regions of the spectrum.
  • Unlike an actinometer, which measures the intensity of radiation, a bolometer measures the total power or energy of the radiation.
  • Bolometers operate based on the principle of detecting changes in temperature caused by the absorption of radiation. A typical bolometer consists of a thermally sensitive element, such as a thin strip of metal or semiconductor material, which is heated by the absorbed radiation. The change in temperature of the element is measured using a temperature sensor, such as a resistance thermometer, and this change is proportional to the power of the incident radiation.
  • Bolometers are widely used in astronomy, spectroscopy, and remote sensing to measure the intensity of radiation emitted by celestial bodies, study the composition of planetary atmospheres, and detect faint signals from distant objects.
